Boot Sequence

Mario’s Sound Lab is designed as a playable synth-design classroom and performance toy. You can play the fixed two-octave piano by touch or with a connected Bluetooth/physical keyboard, switch between iconic sound banks, sculpt patches with studio-style controls, and watch the visual engine react to notes, chords, camera brightness, and motion in real time.

1. Play the piano.
The piano stays pinned to the bottom so it always feels like an instrument. Touch the keys, or pair a Bluetooth/physical keyboard and use it as a mock piano controller.
2. Use the keyboard map.
Tap “Key Map” in the piano dock to open a collapsible reference. Lower octave: Z S X D C V G B H N J M. Upper octave: Q 2 W 3 E R 5 T 6 Y 7 U. Space toggles Sustain.
3. Hold, stack, and sustain.
Stack Chords lets tapped notes stay on for chord building. Sustain works like a pedal: turn it on to let released notes ring, then turn it off to fade them cleanly.
4. Sculpt the sound.
Use tone, attack, release, detune, LFO, drive, phaser, flanger, chorus, delay, and reverb to hear how each parameter reshapes the patch.
5. Open the visual engine.
The square visual display can blend animated audio feedback with a camera-pixel portrait effect. Collapse it any time and reopen it from the small waveform button.
6. Camera expression.
Camera mode uses the browser’s camera stream with permission. Brightness and motion can push air, shimmer, filter, modulation, and delay. Native biometric face sensors stay private and are not exposed to web pages, so this app uses visual camera analysis instead.

Tip: Bluetooth keyboards connect through the device’s normal Bluetooth settings first; the web app then receives normal key events. Camera access works best from HTTPS, such as GitHub Pages. Use Emergency Reset any time audio, camera, sustain, or controls feel stuck.
